Introduction

Modern data centers consume large amounts of electricity for both computing and cooling. As computational demand increases, operators must balance three competing objectives: maintaining service performance, reducing operating cost, and limiting environmental impact.

This study analyzes a structured data center dataset containing hourly workload demand, electricity prices, server parameters, adsorption cooling system operating states, and planning-related variables. The main goal is to show how data analytics and machine learning can support smarter operational planning.

Motivation

Data center operation is influenced by three strongly coupled factors:

  • time-varying computational workload,
  • fluctuating electricity prices,
  • nonlinear cooling-system performance.

These factors do not evolve independently. Peak workload periods may not align with the highest electricity-price hours, and cooling efficiency depends heavily on operating temperatures. This creates a strong opportunity for AI-based control and cost-aware scheduling.

Dataset Overview

The dataset includes multiple sheets describing the operational and physical characteristics of a data center system:

  • Workload Curves: hourly interactive and batch workload demand
  • Electricity Price Curves: hourly electricity price in USD/kWh
  • Server Parameters: server power, speed, frequency, and capacity limits
  • ADS Operating States: cooling-system efficiency and cooling capacity over temperature conditions
  • Planning Parameters: economic planning inputs
  • Carbon Emission: carbon intensity and carbon price
  • Cooling-related Equipment: thermal and equipment parameters
  • Physical Properties: constants such as density and specific heat

The analysis focuses on workload behavior, power demand, electricity cost, emissions, and cooling-system efficiency.

Objectives

The main objectives of this work are:

  • characterize daily workload and electricity-price patterns,
  • estimate derived server and facility power behavior,
  • evaluate hourly cost and carbon implications,
  • analyze the nonlinear performance map of the adsorption cooling system,
  • build an AI surrogate model that predicts cooling efficiency and cooling capacity.

Main AI task: Learn a fast surrogate model for ADS coefficient of performance (COP) and cooling capacity as functions of generator and condenser temperatures.

Methodology

1. Descriptive analytics

Hourly interactive and batch workloads are combined to form total demand. These time profiles are used to understand daily operating patterns.

2. Derived operational metrics

Using the server and cooling parameters, several operational quantities are estimated:

  • required server count,
  • server operating frequency,
  • IT power demand,
  • cooling power demand,
  • total facility power,
  • electricity cost,
  • carbon emissions.

3. Cooling-system analytics

The adsorption cooling system is represented by a performance map with inputs:

  • generator temperature (T^{gen}),
  • condenser temperature (T^{cond}),

and outputs:

  • coefficient of performance (COP),
  • cooling capacity (q^{cap}).

4. AI model

A Random Forest multi-output regression model is trained to predict both COP and cooling capacity from operating temperatures.

Discussion

This study highlights three main insights.

1. Predictable workload structure

The workload follows a strong and interpretable daily pattern. This makes forecasting and adaptive scheduling feasible.

2. Cost-aware opportunity

Electricity prices do not perfectly align with workload demand. This mismatch creates room for cost reduction through smarter task allocation, especially for flexible workloads such as batch processing.

3. Nonlinear cooling behavior

Cooling performance is highly nonlinear. Simple linear rules are not sufficient to describe the relationship between operating temperatures, efficiency, and cooling capacity. The Random Forest surrogate provides a practical alternative by learning these relationships directly from data.

Conclusion

This work demonstrates how statistical analysis and machine learning can support smarter data center planning and operation.

The main findings are:

  • workload and electricity-price profiles reveal meaningful scheduling opportunities,
  • facility power, cost, and carbon emissions vary substantially over the day,
  • ADS performance is strongly nonlinear,
  • a Random Forest surrogate accurately predicts both COP and cooling capacity.

Overall, AI-driven operational analytics can help data centers become more energy-efficient, cost-aware, and environmentally responsible.

Future Work

Potential next steps include:

  • workload forecasting using time-series models,
  • cost-aware batch scheduling,
  • multi-objective optimization of energy, cost, and carbon emissions,
  • reinforcement learning for adaptive control,
  • integration of AI surrogates into digital twins and real-time operational decision systems.
